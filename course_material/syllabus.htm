Course Description

Additional Course Description

Java is one of the most popular general purpose modern programming languages used in industry. This course focuses on learning and practicing advanced Java features which are widely used in industry and academia. The first part of the course is dedicated to object-oriented features of Java and how to use them in software design. It also covers all major tools that support development process during the entire software development life cycle. The second part of the course covers parallel programming, how it can be implemented in Java, its advantages and limitations. The third part of the course is dedicated to creating Web applications. Finally, the fourth part of the course discusses different database models and provides specific details on how to create Java applications that can use different databases as a data storage option. Special attention is given to noSQL graph databases which combined with Java allow building solutions that are capable of storing, manipulating, and analyzing billion-node scale networks.

Course Goals

Goals

This course teaches all major advanced features of Java SE (versions 8 and up) programming language enabling students to understand existing and create their own Java code to solve a wide range of common real-world problems. Special attention is given to designing rich GUI applications, as well as network enabled and high performance solutions. Basic features of Java EE are covered in the context of creating Web applications. Designing and implementing data-centric applications is introduced with the JDBC framework and noSQL graph database Neo4j.

Course (Student) Learning Outcomes Assessment Measures

In-class exercises

In-class practice exercises will be offered in almost every lecture. They are not graded and can be completed individually or in small groups.

Article Review

Each student will select an article (paper, blog, book chapter) to review from the list of materials provided by the instructor. The review will be prepared as a written document and an oral in-class presentation. The written review should restate in student's own words all essential ideas from the original article and discuss student's own attitude toward the topic of the article. The drafts of the written review and presentation (slides) are submitted for the approval of the instructor prior to presentation. A student will incorporate instructor feedback into the final versions of the written review and presentation (slides) which will be submitted for a grade, in addition to making an oral in-class presentation.

The expected length of the written review is at least two pages (~1000 words). The expected length of the oral presentation is about 5-7 minutes.

Homework Assignments

There are five homework assignments. All homework assignments must be completed individually unless stated otherwise.

Homework assignments will include one or more written documents that describe the testing strategy, test cases, experimental results, and analysis of the results and performance. Written parts of all assignments will add up to at least 4,000 words.

Homework assignments will have a peer grading component when students will be required to write detailed, well structured, and meaningful reviews assessing the work done by others.

Assignments are due at 11:59 pm Eastern Time (EST or EDT) on the due date. Students are given five late days for the entire semester that they can use at their discretion but with a limit of no more than two late days per assignment. Late days can only be applied to homework assignments.

Final project (team project)

Around the middle of the semester students will form teams of two to four people and start selecting a topic for the final project. For the final project, students will implement a software solution, write a report documenting their work and describing the results, and make an in-class presentation.

The expected length of the written report is 2-8 pages (~1,000-4,000 words) depending on the project and the size of the team. All members of the team must have a part in the presentation, including answering follow-up questions from other students and teaching staff. The expected length of the oral presentation is about 5-7 minutes per person.

Grading Criteria

Criteria Details

The final course grade is composed of the following:

·         Homework assignments - 50% (10% each assignment)

·         Article review (5%) and presentation (5%).

·         Final team project - 35% (21% implementation, 7% written report, and 7% presentation)

·         Participation in peer grading experience - 5% (awarded if completed 80% or more of the assigned peer grading, 0 otherwise). Completing a peer grading assignment implies leaving detailed, well structured, and meaningful written comments regarding the work being reviewed. Academic Integrity

Student-teacher relationships are built on trust. For example, students must trust that teachers have made appropriate decisions about the structure and content of the courses they teach, and teachers must trust that the assignments that students turn in are their own. Acts that violate this trust undermine the educational process. The Rensselaer Handbook of Student Rights and Responsibilities and The Graduate Student Supplement define various forms of Academic Dishonesty and you should make yourself familiar with these. In this class, all assignments that are turned in for a grade must represent the student's own work. In cases where help was received, or teamwork was allowed, a notation on the assignment should indicate your collaboration. 

Violations of academic integrity may also be reported to the appropriate Dean (Dean of Students for undergraduate students or the Dean of Graduate Education for graduate students, respectively).

If you have any question concerning this policy before submitting an assignment, please ask for clarification. In addition, you can visit the following site for more information on our Academic Integrity Policy: Students Rights, Responsibilities, and Judicial Affairs.

Additional Academic Integrity Course Policy and Penalty Information

Assignments must be completed individually. Any part of the work that has been created by someone else (e.g., using a library function, API, or framework) must not constitute more than 10% of the entire assignment or project and must be clearly attributed. Referencing or reusing any work created by someone else must be permitted by relevant licenses and comply with all applicable laws and regulations.

We encourage discussion, however, carrying out material (e.g., photos, writing) from a discussion is not allowed. The actual work must be your own. Submitty has advanced plagiarism detection capabilities, which we will use on every homework assignment. Excessive similarities between different submissions, including past semester submissions, will result in a grade of zero for the corresponding assignment or project in addition to one letter grade penalty for all students involved, and a referral to the Dean of Students. A subsequent infraction will result in an automatic grade of F for the course.
